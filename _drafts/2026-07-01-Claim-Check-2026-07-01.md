# Claim Check: General Fusion’s LM26 compressional-heating result

**Verdict:** Progress, but still a sub-keV plasma result: LM26 showed compression and electron heating, but not to 1 keV or 10 keV fuel-ion temperature or Lawson-relevant performance.

## What happened

On June 22, 2026, [General Fusion announced](https://generalfusion.com/post/general-fusion-achieves-compressional-plasma-heating-with-lm26-magnetized-target-fusion-machine/) that its [LM26 magnetized target fusion machine](https://generalfusion.com/path-to-commercialization/lm26-program/) had achieved compressional plasma heating. The company reported an electron temperature of about 0.72 keV during lithium-liner compression.

The pre-peer review [technical paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23974) presents results from the first 11 LM26 compression shots. The best shots showed more than a 3× rise in electron temperature, about a 10× rise in density, and about a 10× rise in poloidal magnetic field during roughly 3× radial compression.

[Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering), the gold standard of electron temperature measurements was used with the best results on shot LMC-11: 0.72 keV; another indepdendent measurement (UV photodiodes) gave 0.67 keV. The fuel-ion result was lower. Neutron-inferred deuterium ion temperatures during compression were about 0.41 keV for LMC-11.

## What is actually new

This is the first detailed public data from LM26 lithium-liner compression shots: measured liner compression, magnetic-field amplification, density rise, electron-temperature rise, neutron and gamma signals, MHD behavior, and transport modeling. It's a well diagnosed and analyzed set of experiments.

The data support the narrow claim that compressional heating occurred, especially for electrons. Importantly, they also show that LM26 came far short of reaching fusion-relevant ion temperatures.

## The claim beneath the claim

The claim beneath the claim is that LM26 has validated the central [Magnetized Target Fusion](https://generalfusion.com/fusion-technology/) bet: form a magnetized spherical tokamak plasma at moderate temperature, mechanically compress it fast enough with a lithium liner, conserve particles and magnetic flux well enough, and let adiabatic compression carry the plasma to fusion-relevant conditions.

If compression is self-similar and particle-conserving, then density should scale like inverse volume:

$$
\frac{n}{n_0} \approx \frac{V_0}{V} \approx C_R^3
$$

where $C_R$ is the radial-equivalent compression ratio (i.e., the ratio of the initial plasma radius to the final plasma radius).

For plasma heated adiabatically (without energy loss),

$$
\frac{T}{T_0} \approx \left(\frac{V_0}{V}\right)^{2/3} \approx C_R^2
$$

A 3× radial compression should ideally give:

$$
\frac{n}{n_0} \approx 3^3 = 27
$$

and

$$
\frac{T}{T_0} \approx 3^2 = 9.
$$

Starting from a 0.21 keV electron plasma, a lossless 3× radial compression would ideally reach about 1.9 keV.

LM26 instead reached up to 0.72 keV electron temperature, about 0.4 keV deuterium ion temperature, and about 10× density increase.

While that is compression and heating, it is far short of adiabatic compression.

## What would have to be true

- **Ion heating must become much closer to adiabatic.** The paper’s ion-temperature model has the form

  $$
  \frac{T_i(t)}{T_i(0)}
  =
  \left[\frac{V(0)}{V(t)}\right]^{2/3}
  \exp\left[
  -\hat{\chi}_i
  \int_0^t k(t')^2\,dt'
  \right].
  $$

  The first term is the desired adiabatic heating. The exponential term is the loss penalty from ion heat transport. The paper finds that an effective ion heat transport of $\hat{\chi}_i \simeq 4\ \mathrm{m^2/s}$ is enough to prevent significant ion heating, while $\hat{\chi}_i \simeq 2\ \mathrm{m^2/s}$ would have produced a stronger ion temperature rise. In plain English: the plasma leaked heat too fast.

- **The compression ratio must be judged against the starting temperature.** For ideal adiabatic heating,

  $$
  C_R = \sqrt{\frac{T_f}{T_0}}
  $$

  and

  $$
  \frac{V_0}{V} = \left(\frac{T_f}{T_0}\right)^{3/2}.
  $$

  Using the reported starting electron temperatures, LM26 did not need an absurd compression ratio to reach 1 keV in the ideal case. From 0.21 keV, 1 keV requires only

  $$
  C_R \approx \sqrt{\frac{1000}{210}} \approx 2.18
  $$

  or about 10× volume compression. 

  LM26 reached geometric compression in that range (up to about 3). The reason it did not reach 1 keV was not that the liner failed to compress the plasma enough. It was because the plasma did not conserve heat and particles well enough.

- **Reaching 10 keV is much harder.** From a 0.21 keV starting plasma, adiabatic heating to 10 keV requires

  $$
  C_R \approx \sqrt{\frac{10000}{210}} \approx 6.9
  $$

  or about 330× volume compression.

  From a 0.30 keV deuterium ion temperature**, reaching 10 keV ideally requires

  $$
  C_R \approx \sqrt{\frac{10000}{300}} \approx 5.8
  $$

  or about 190× volume compression.

  These are lossless estimates. Real compression requires either better confinement, higher starting temperature, larger compression, faster compression, or some combination of them.

- **The confinement time must improve relative to the compression time.** General Fusion has previously pointed to energy-confinement times exceeding 10 ms as the target-plasma lifetime needed for LM26’s compression program.

  In LMC-11, the inferred ion energy confinement time was about 5.3 ms, while the compression time was about 3.6 ms, giving

  $$
  \frac{\tau_{E,i}}{\tau_C} \approx 1.5.
  $$

  That is enough to see some late ion reheating, but not enough for clean adiabatic ion heating to keV-scale fuel-ion temperatures.

## What is still missing

The missing result is not “any heating.” LM26 showed heating.

The missing result is **convincing, simultaneous, fusion-relevant fuel-plasma performance**.

Specifically, the next evidence package should show **1 keV electron temperature by robust diagnostics**, **fuel-ion temperature approaching or exceeding 1 keV from neutron-inferred deuterium data**, and **density rise attributable to deuterium fuel rather than late impurity influx**.

The distinction matters because the paper reports near-1 keV ion Doppler spikes in LMC-8 and LMC-9, but attributes them to preferential carbon-ion heating from reconnection, not bulk deuterium fuel-ion heating. The neutron data do not show a simultaneous deuterium temperature spike.

Also missing is a shot where the carbon impurity ion temperature, electron temperature, neutron-inferred deuterium ion temperature, and density reconstruction all tell the same story.

Finally, the upgrades need to demonstrate that the loss term can be beaten. The paper points to higher magnetic field and improved shaping, including negative triangularity, as routes to lower ion transport and stronger self-similar compression. General Fusion has also discussed diagnostic and temperature-measurement work for LM26 in a separate [March 2026 publication announcement](https://generalfusion.com/post/new-peer-reviewed-publication-demonstrates-diagnostic-method-for-measuring-target-temperatures-in-large-scale-lm26-fusion-machine/). That is a plausible plan, but it is not yet a demonstrated LM26 result.

## Why it matters

If General Fusion can make this work, it matters because mechanical compression is the whole point of the MTF approach. The power-plant promise is not just “make a hot plasma.” It is to make a magnetized target that can be heated primarily by moving metal walls, potentially avoiding some of the scale and cost penalties of high-field superconducting tokamaks or laser-driven inertial fusion.

This result supports part of that thesis. LM26 compressed a magnetized plasma, amplified magnetic field, increased density, increased electron temperature, and produced a neutron and gamma increase during compression.

It does not yet establish the harder part: that the **fuel ions** can be compressed fast enough and cleanly enough to run up to the 1–10 keV regime before transport, MHD activity, and wall interaction take the energy away.

## Bottom line

This is **real but limited**.

LM26 has demonstrated compressional heating, but the published result is still a sub-keV electron-temperature result and a roughly **0.3–0.4 keV deuterium ion-temperature** result. The machine did not fail to move the liner. It failed to make the plasma behave adiabatically enough, especially for the fuel ions.

The next observable milestone is straightforward: a repeatable LM26 shot with **≥1 keV electron temperature**, a corresponding **neutron-inferred deuterium ion temperature moving toward 1 keV**, density rise that is clearly fuel compression rather than impurity influx, and stable compression beyond the present $C_R \sim 3$ class of shots.

Without that, the path from this result to **10 keV** remains a transport-and-stability extrapolation, not an experimental demonstration.
