[LIthium BReeding Tritium Innovation program (LIBRTI)](https://www.ukaea.org/work/librti/) is a high flux D-T neutron source to test tritium breeding in different blanket materials. Ironic that a US company announces a collaboration with the UK on an experiment with a name that sounds like *liberty* nearly 250 years to the day of the signing of the Declaration of Independence of the US from the UK. 

[Realta claims first direct energy conversion](https://realtafusion.com/fusion-first-realta-demos-direct-energy-conversion/). The system gathered "multiple amps of current at around 100 volts". This feels to me like Realta did this entirely to beat Helion to be the first, not because it was a critical demonstration. Direct energy conversion is not new in mirrors, having been [considered](https://inis.iaea.org/records/ae6cf-tbn64/files/6176387.pdf) for many decades and [first demonstrated](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/fusion-science-and-technology.pdf) at least 45 years ago. As I stated last time this came up with Helion: basically every fusion experiment has had some sort of "direct energy" conversion from the input and fusion energy in the plasma to electricity. That's what most (all?) measurement systems do. The largest likely conversion of energy in a plasma to electricity is with the central solenoid in tokamaks when the plasma current is ramped down

- [UKAEA and Eni launch RH3OVA to advance fusion industry](https://www.ukaea.org/news/) UKAEA and Eni said on July 2 that they had formed RH3OVA, a joint venture to provide consultancy and operational services around fusion fuel-cycle bottlenecks, especially tritium handling and related systems. What is new in the window is not another memorandum but the formal launch of an operating vehicle aimed at selling services into the emerging fusion market. That matters because fuel-cycle capability is one of the hardest gating items for plant licensing, procurement, and bankable delivery, so a dedicated UKAEA-Eni platform strengthens the investability of the broader fusion supply chain. The qualification is that RH3OVA is an enabling-services move rather than a reactor-build decision.

- [LIBRTI: The Lithium Breeding Tritium Innovation Programme](https://ccfe.ukaea.uk/programmes/fusion-futures/librti/) UKAEA said on July 1 that Commonwealth Fusion Systems had become the first international company to join LIBRTI, its flagship program for testing tritium-breeding blanket technologies. The genuinely new development is CFS crossing from bilateral rhetoric into a named testing program tied to one of fusion’s central commercialization hurdles: breeding and managing its own fuel. That matters for investors, utilities, and industrial customers because blanket and tritium work is a key measure of whether reactor developers are closing the gap between plasma progress and power-plant readiness. The limitation is that this is still a development and test milestone, not proof of an integrated commercial blanket.

- [General Fusion and Renexia Announce Framework Agreement for the Commercial Deployment of Fusion Power in Italy](https://generalfusion.com/post/general-fusion-and-renexia-announce-framework-agreement-for-the-commercial-deployment-of-fusion-power-in-italy/) General Fusion said on June 24 that it had signed a milestone-based framework agreement with Renexia to pursue the siting, development, funding, construction, and commissioning of one or more fusion plants in Italy. What is new in the window is a concrete commercial-entry framework with a local renewable-energy and infrastructure counterparty in a major European market, rather than another generic partnership announcement. That matters because fusion developers need credible local partners to move from demonstration machines to actual projects with sites, permits, and financing pathways. This is company-announced, and the agreement is still a framework rather than a final investment commitment.

- [Fusion supply chain spend up, but challenges remain](https://www.world-nuclear-news.org/articles/fusion-supply-chain-spend-up-big-challenges-remain) World Nuclear News reported on June 23 that the Fusion Industry Association’s 2026 supply-chain report showed private-sector fusion supply-chain spending rising to $538 million in 2025, with respondents projecting another jump in 2026. The new development in the window is the release of fresh market data pointing to faster industrial scaling even as firms still flag shortages in specialist materials, manufacturing capacity, and fuel-cycle capabilities. That matters for suppliers, infrastructure providers, and investors because it offers one of the clearest recent reads on where fusion spending is actually moving from lab hardware toward industrial procurement. The caveat is that the survey reflects self-reported data from only about half of private fusion companies, so it is directional rather than comprehensive.
