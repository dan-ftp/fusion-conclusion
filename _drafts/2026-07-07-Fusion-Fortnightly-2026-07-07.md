[UKAEA and Eni launch RH3OVA, a colaboration on fusion fuel cycle](https://www.eni.com/en-IT/media/press-release/2026/07/pr-ukaea-eni-launch-rh3ova-expedite-fusion-energy-industrialisation.html). [CFS named first international partner in UKAEA’s LIBRTI programme](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/cfs-named-first-international-partner-in-ukaeas-librti-programme). [LIthium BReeding Tritium Innovation program (LIBRTI)](https://www.ukaea.org/work/librti/) is a high flux D-T neutron source to test tritium breeding in different blanket materials. Ironic that a US company announces a collaboration with the UK on an experiment with a name that sounds like *liberty* nearly 250 years to the day of the signing of the Declaration of Independence of the US from the UK. 

[Realta claims first direct energy conversion](https://realtafusion.com/fusion-first-realta-demos-direct-energy-conversion/). The system gathered "multiple amps of current at around 100 volts". This feels to me like Realta did this entirely to beat Helion to be the first, not because it was a critical demonstration. Direct energy conversion is not new in mirrors, having been [considered](https://inis.iaea.org/records/ae6cf-tbn64/files/6176387.pdf) for many decades and [first demonstrated](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/fusion-science-and-technology.pdf) at least 45 years ago. As I stated last time this came up with Helion: basically every fusion experiment has had some sort of "direct energy" conversion from the input and fusion energy in the plasma to electricity. That's what most (all?) measurement systems do. The largest likely conversion of energy in a plasma to electricity is with the central solenoid in tokamaks when the plasma current is ramped down at [JET with a 7 MA plasma](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.13182/FST89-A39714), which is about 50 MJ of stored inductive energy at probably about 1 MW or so.

- [Integral Nuclear Data and Benchmarking Needs for Fusion Energy Systems](https://www.ans.org/pubs/journals/fst/article-60534/). Category: Neutronics, materials qualification, and nuclear QA. Confidence: High. Summary: Published on 1 July 2026 in *Fusion Science and Technology*, this paper argues that fusion designs are already leaning on radiation-transport models for novel materials and tritium-breeding systems, but that publicly benchmarked integral data remain too sparse. The authors say only a few groups globally are producing relevant integral data and that substantial investment is needed to meet the validation needs of the fusion community. Fusion relevance: This is a sharp industrialization signal because qualification bottlenecks will not be solved by design software alone; they depend on benchmark experiments, data infrastructure, and validation workflows that suppliers, regulators, and insurers can trust. - [Uncertainty Analyses of Tritium Production and Gamma Heating Rates in the FNS Clean Benchmark Experiments](https://www.ans.org/pubs/journals/fst/article-60539/). Category: Neutronics, materials qualification, and nuclear QA. Confidence: High. Summary: Published on 1 July 2026 in *Fusion Science and Technology*, this paper quantified nuclear-data uncertainty propagation for fusion benchmarks across vanadium, beryllium, tungsten, iron, copper, and graphite assemblies irradiated by a 14-MeV D-T source. The authors found gamma-heating uncertainty up to 23% in vanadium, about 20% in graphite, 17% in tungsten, 14% in iron, and less than 6% in copper, while tritium-production uncertainty in beryllium assemblies was dominated by beryllium cross sections rather than lithium-6 impurities. Fusion relevance: This matters because large uncertainty bands translate directly into heavier design margins, more conservative shielding, more testing, and harder qualification for blankets and structural materials. It is a technical paper, but the commercial signal is simple: design confidence in several fusion-relevant materials is still less mature than most deployment timelines imply. citeturn27view2
