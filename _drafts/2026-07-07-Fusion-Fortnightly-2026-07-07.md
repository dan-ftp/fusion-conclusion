[Realta claims first direct energy conversion](https://realtafusion.com/fusion-first-realta-demos-direct-energy-conversion/). The system gathered "multiple amps of current at around 100 volts". This feels to me like Realta did this entirely to beat Helion to be the first, not because it was a critical demonstration. Direct energy conversion is not new in mirrors, having been [considered](https://inis.iaea.org/records/ae6cf-tbn64/files/6176387.pdf) for many decades and [first demonstrated](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/fusion-science-and-technology.pdf) at least 45 years ago. As I stated last time this came up with Helion: basically every fusion experiment has had some sort of "direct energy" conversion from the input and fusion energy in the plasma to electricity. That's what most (all?) measurement systems do. The largest likely conversion of energy in a plasma to electricity is with the central solenoid in tokamaks when the plasma current is ramped down at [JET with a 7 MA plasma](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.13182/FST89-A39714), which is about 50 MJ of stored inductive energy at probably about 1 MW or so.

[STEP Fusion: Overview of the Concept Design for the Spherical Tokamak for Energy Production and Lessons Learnt](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10894-026-00589-5) published in Journal of Fusion Energy. STEP still aims for a spherical tokamak (it's baked in the name) by 2040 that can produce at least 100 MWe net and be tritium fuel self-sufficient. The major update from SPP-1 to SPP-2 is due to center-column magnet lifetime and blanket feasibility. The major radius increased from 3.6 m to 4.275 m, raising the projected centre-column lifetime from 0.22 to 0.6 full-power years. This is a common trend as the design fidelity increases, important issues like neutron sheilding reveal themselves to be harder as design fidelity increases (more holes!). The breader blanket changed from liquid lithium to a solid lithium oxide.

[Tungsten prices have nearly 10x since the start of the year](https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-opinion-australia-tungsten-mine-us-war-defense-china/). Of course, 80% of the present tungsten supply comes from China (another 5% from Russia and North Korea). Tungsten is useful for fusion as the plasma-facing components because of the excellent thermal properties, low transmutation, low tritium retention, and low sputtering. Tungsten is useful in war because it is dense, hard, and heat resistant. Tungsten used in fusion can likely be recycled; tungsten used in war is likely lost. Recent conflicts have increased the tungsten consumption. Taking the early [ARC design] as an example, it estimated that tungsten was about 0.025% of the total cost, so a 10x increase does not strongly affect the bottom line for fusion.
