# Claim Check: Realta Fusion [claims](https://realtafusion.com/fusion-first-realta-demos-direct-energy-conversion/) to be the first private fusion company in history to demonstrate direct energy conversion of plasma kinetic energy into electricity

**Verdict:** A genuine power-recovery demonstration wrapped in an indefensible literal first. A private fusion experiment converted charged fusion-product energy into electrical detector signals at least as early as 1977. 

## What happened

On June 30, Realta announced that it had demonstrated direct energy conversion on the Wisconsin HTS Axisymmetric Mirror (WHAM) during experiments on June 19. Charged particles escaping through the mirror ends passed through two grids and reached a positively biased ion collector. Slowing and collecting the ions produced “multiple amps” at around 100 volts.

Realta was clear that this was not net fusion electricity. Nearly all the recovered energy had first been supplied by WHAM’s plasma-heating systems.

## What is actually new

Direct energy conversion has already been done in magnetic mirrors: Electrostatic direct conversion in mirrors was first done at Lawrence Livermore began work on plasma direct converters in the 1970s with tests up to [100 kilovolts and 6 kilowatts](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/fusion-science-and-technology.pdf). In 1983 a converter on the Tandem Mirror Experiment recovered [67 watts from approximately 140 watts of incident ion power](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/fusion-science-and-technology.pdf), for a measured efficiency of 48% and a separate 22-stage experimental converter had previously reported approximately 86% electrostatic recovery. 

Realta’s claim is narrower: that no *private* fusion company had previously converted plasma kinetic energy into electricity. Putting on my pendantic physicist hat, that is false as written.

### The Migmacell II counterexample

While there may be counterexamples that predate the following, this was the best evidence I could find to satisfy the claim of "the first private fusion company in history to successfully demonstrate direct energy conversion (DEC) of plasma kinetic energy into electricity."

In 1977, the Migma Institute and Fusion Energy Corporation published [“Fusion products detection system in Migmacell II”](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0029554X77900489). They used three silicon surface-barrier detectors to measure protons and tritons from D–D reactions involving 0.55 MeV deuterons. The charged fusion products deposited kinetic energy in the silicon, creating electron-hole pairs and electrical charge pulses. Fusion Energy Corporation had therefore converted charged-particle kinetic energy into electricity 49 years before Realta’s announcement. The output was microscopic and diagnostic. 

## The claim beneath the claim

The real claim is that a magnetic mirror’s principal weakness—particles escaping through its open ends—could become an advantage.

A mirror reactor could direct escaping ions into an expander, separate electrons from ions and electrostatically slow the ions onto biased collectors. This could recover some alpha-particle energy and recycle part of the neutral-beam or radio-frequency power used to heat the plasma.

Realta assumes that 80% of D–T fusion output would pass through a thermal cycle operating at up to 45% efficiency, while the remaining 20% would pass through direct conversion at more than 90% efficiency. The company says this could offset its injected heating power and [lower electricity costs by 10–20%](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/realta-fusion-becomes-first-commercial-fusion-company-to-convert-plasma-energy-into-electricity-302813632.html).

Those claims go far beyond lighting several bulbs.

## What would have to be true

* A large fraction of alpha and injected heating power must leave axially as collectable charged particles.
* The converter must remain efficient across broad particle energies and pitch angles.
* Grids, collectors and insulators must survive high voltage, particle flux, radiation, sputtering and plasma contamination.
* The converter must not undermine the plasma potentials and expander geometry needed for confinement and stability.
* Net recovered electricity must exceed auxiliary loads, added capital cost and maintenance losses.
* The full gross-to-net plant balance must improve.

## What is still missing

Realta has not published the incident ion and electron power, electrical energy recovered per shot, suppressor and bias power, rejected heat or net converter efficiency.

Nor has it separated energy originating from plasma heating, stored plasma energy and fusion reactions. The fusion contribution was presumably negligible.

The older mirror literature shows why scale matters. Barr and Moir found that background-gas ionization and secondary electrons became more important as power increased. They also identified high-voltage breakdown and the stored energy of large converters as unresolved concerns.

A [2017 mirror-reactor review](https://www.ralphmoir.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/08/Fowler_2017_Nucl._Fusion_57_056014.pdf) used 50% direct-conversion efficiency as its working assumption. It discussed roughly 100-kilovolt systems for D–T exhaust, estimated a grid power-density limit near 1 megawatt per square meter and noted that converter electrodes can affect the plasma potentials needed for stability and radial transport.

Direct conversion reduces the economic penalty from plasma end losses. It does not improve confinement or increase scientific fusion gain.

## Why it matters

Mirrors are unusually suited to direct conversion because they naturally channel escaping charged particles toward their ends.

At low fusion gain, a converter could recycle some externally supplied heating power. At high gain, it could recover part of the alpha-particle energy. But in D–T fusion, approximately 80% of fusion energy appears in neutrons and only 20% in charged alpha particles. Most of the plant must still use a blanket and thermal cycle.

Direct conversion could materially improve a D–T plant, but it cannot transform its entire output into electricity at 90% efficiency.

## What the numbers imply

Let (\eta_{\rm DC}) be direct-conversion efficiency and (\eta_{\rm th}) the efficiency of the thermal cycle. If unrecovered converter power becomes useful heat, the effective efficiency of the converter stream is

[
\eta_{\rm stream}
=================

\eta_{\rm DC}
+
(1-\eta_{\rm DC})\eta_{\rm th}.
]

If (f_{\rm DC}) is the fraction of total fusion power reaching the converter, gross plant efficiency is

[
\eta_{\rm gross}
================

\eta_{\rm th}
+
f_{\rm DC}\eta_{\rm DC}(1-\eta_{\rm th}).
]

The improvement over an all-thermal plant is therefore

[
\Delta\eta_{\rm gross}
======================

f_{\rm DC}\eta_{\rm DC}(1-\eta_{\rm th}).
]

Using Realta’s optimistic assumptions,

[
f_{\rm DC}=0.20,\qquad
\eta_{\rm th}=0.45,\qquad
\eta_{\rm DC}=0.90,
]

the directly convertible stream has an effective efficiency of

[
\eta_{\rm stream}
=================

# 0.90+0.10(0.45)

0.945.
]

Whole-plant gross efficiency becomes

[
\eta_{\rm gross}
================

# 0.80(0.45)+0.20(0.945)

0.549.
]

Thus 90% direct conversion of the entire alpha-energy stream raises gross efficiency from 45% to 54.9%, not to 90%.

For a 1,000 MW fusion source:

[
800\ {\rm MW}\times0.45=360\ {\rm MWe},
]

[
200\ {\rm MW}\times0.90=180\ {\rm MWe},
]

[
200\ {\rm MW}\times0.10\times0.45=9\ {\rm MWe}.
]

Total gross output is therefore

[
360+180+9=549\ {\rm MWe},
]

compared with 450 MWe for an all-thermal plant.

Net power must still subtract plasma heating. Let

[
Q=\frac{P_{\rm fusion}}{P_{\rm heat}}
]

and let (\eta_{\rm heat}) be heating-system wall-plug efficiency. Ignoring other loads,

[
\frac{P_{\rm net}}{P_{\rm fusion}}
==================================

## \eta_{\rm gross}

\frac{1}{Q\eta_{\rm heat}}.
]

The simplified break-even gain is

[
Q_{\rm break-even}
==================

\frac{1}{\eta_{\rm heat}\eta_{\rm gross}}.
]

At an illustrative 50% heating efficiency, direct conversion lowers simplified break-even (Q) from

[
\frac{1}{0.50\times0.45}=4.44
]

to

[
\frac{1}{0.50\times0.549}=3.64.
]

That is useful, but it does not rescue a poor-confinement reactor by itself.

Realta also hopes to recover injected heating power. If (r_{\rm heat}) is the fraction returned as net electricity and (p_{\rm other}) represents other plant loads,

[
\frac{P_{\rm net}}{P_{\rm fusion}}
==================================

## \eta_{\rm gross}

## \frac{1/\eta_{\rm heat}-r_{\rm heat}}{Q}

p_{\rm other}.
]

This is the balance Realta must ultimately close. Its experiment shows only that (r_{\rm heat}>0) under one set of WHAM conditions.

## Bottom line

Realta performed a real and worthwhile experiment, but wrote the historical claim too broadly.

Fusion Energy Corporation had already converted charged D–D fusion-product energy into electrical detector pulses in 1977. Realta’s stronger claim is that it recovered useful power from bulk mirror-plasma exhaust and drove an external load. That appears plausible and is the achievement it should have advertised.

The next meaningful milestone is a reproducible kilowatt-scale result with a closed power balance: incident plasma power, auxiliary consumption, rejected heat, net electrical output and efficiency. After that must come operation at reactor-relevant voltage, power density and duty factor.

Until then, the result is worth watching but does not materially strengthen Realta’s reactor case.
