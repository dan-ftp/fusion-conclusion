China just released its 15th five-year plan and fusion is a prominent target industry that the CCP intends to dominate. This should worry investors who have poured billions into US fusion startups. Will your fusion investment be to China Fusion Inc. what Tesla is becoming to BYD?

This concern is real. Out of 35 future-industry technology families targeted in China’s five-year-plan, China has reached more than 30% global share in 11. With that group 6 technologies are at a 70% or higher share: solar PV manufacturing, lithium-ion battery supply-chain capacity, electric-vehicle production, selected battery-material processing, commercial drones, and shipbuilding. If fusion follows the trajectory of these Chinese-dominated industries, private sector investment in US fusion companies will not deliver the outsized returns that many funds are betting on.

But China hasn’t always won. After three decades of attempting to build a commercial aviation sector, Chinese companies delivered about 1% of total aircraft last year, mostly to its domestic market. Does the fusion industry have more in common with electric vehicle or with the commercial aviation? The answer may well determine who wins the race to economically-viable fusion energy at-scale.

## How China’s five-year planning system turns priorities into industries

It is worth understanding how China’s five-year planning process works because it is the main instruments by which China has built global industries. The plans are often described in the West as long policy documents, but that understates their function. China’s [five-year plans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-year_plans_of_China) are tools of state direction. They set priorities, create bureaucratic obligations, direct capital, and give lower levels of government the map of what Beijing wants.

The system began in the 1950s. China’s First Five-Year Plan covered 1953 to 1957 and drew heavily from Soviet-style industrial planning. Its original emphasis was heavy industry, state ownership, and basic industrial capacity. Over time, the plans changed with the economy. Under reform and opening, they became less like command plans and more like structured guidance for an economy with markets, private firms, foreign capital, and local experimentation. The Party still sets the direction, but implementation now runs through a mix of ministries, provinces, cities, state firms, private firms, universities, banks, funds, and industrial parks.

The process starts well before a plan is published. The center reviews past performance, commissions research, consults ministries, provinces, think tanks, universities, firms, and technical experts, and then produces a hierarchy of documents. The national plan sits at the top. Beneath it come ministry plans, provincial plans, municipal plans, sector plans, technology roadmaps, implementation notices, procurement programs, funding schemes, and local industrial policies. Vivienne Shue has called the process one of “comprehensive, long-term, cross-sectoral, macro-planning.” 

That hierarchy matters. A national phrase can become a ministry program. A ministry program can become a provincial target. A provincial target can become a city-level subsidy, a land allocation, a research center, a bank loan, a tax preference, or a procurement order. By the time an industry appears in a national five-year plan, it has often entered a political and financial pipeline. The plan does not guarantee success, but it changes the odds by telling the state where to place attention, money, land, people, and institutional effort.

Competition is central to the model. China does not usually pick one company, one city, or one technical path. It encourages many local governments, firms, laboratories, and industrial parks to chase the same strategic priority. On the downside that creates waste, duplication, and overcapacity. It also creates competition: Local officials compete for projects. Firms compete for subsidies, permits, talent, and market share. Provinces compete to become the national base for a favored technology. The center can then observe which regions, firms, and approaches perform best, reinforce the winners, and let weaker efforts fade, merge, or fail.

Career incentives give the system force. Chinese plans contain targets and assessment criteria. Local officials are evaluated on whether they deliver on these metrics. When a technology becomes politically important, it enters the promotion logic of the Party-state. This career link helps explain why Chinese planning documents often matter more than comparable strategy papers in democratic systems.

Government investment funds add another layer. China has built a large system of state-backed industrial funds that channel public capital into favored sectors. They often use state money to draw in banks, local governments, state firms, private investors, and corporate capital. In strategic sectors, they can support early manufacturing capacity, help firms survive long development cycles, and push private investors toward areas that the Party has marked as important. The result is a financing system that sustains parallel bets before markets alone would do so.

Tech parks and industrial clusters turn the plans into physical ecosystems. It is well known from places like Silicon Valley in the US that concentration of industry in a single location is a force multiplier. In China these parks provide land, tax benefits, infrastructure, permitting help, recruitment support, and proximity to local customers and suppliers. 

All of this machinery helps explain China’s repeated success in scaling technology industries. The five-year plan names the priority. Ministries turn it into programs. Provinces and cities compete to execute. Officials face career pressure to show results. State-backed funds lower the capital barrier. Tech parks gather firms and suppliers. Markets then sort quality, cost, and demand across a large field of competitors. The model can misfire, especially where the technology is harder than expected, where global incumbents are deeply entrenched, or where domestic demand cannot absorb the output. But when China can combine policy direction, local competition, patient capital, manufacturing depth, and a large home market, the five-year planning system becomes a powerful engine for industrial catch-up.

## The Past and Present of China's Fusion Efforts


## China's Speed Advantage

One of the big advantages for China is speed of execution. This is supported by having a strong domestic manufacturing and construction based. [It is also somehow enabled by the management structure]

Contrasting against ITER is unfair, as ITER will go down in history as most poorly set up and managed fusion, if not big science, collaborations. Also, the poor performance of US government in recent fusion projects, including NSTX-U and MPEX, have demonstrated that the US is unable to execute on even relatively modest fusion experiments.

US fusion startups on the other hand are executing in the same league as China. They are pushed forward by the demands of investors, who are less forgiving to long, conservative timelines. This, like the party promotion structure in China, helps insentivize taking measured risks to make projects go faster.

CFS, in collaboration with MIT, developed the Toroidal Field Model Coil for SPARC as a three-year program and got it done only 3 months behind schedule, including delays due to executing the project during the first year and a half of the COVID pandemic. The US government estimated it would have taken a decade to execute on such a program.

Part of the aparent advantage to the many of the startups is that they are executing on small to moderate scale fusion R&D facilities. All have aspirations to scale up (with some thinking they can be successful at a relatively small scale). But only CFS is building at a world-class fusion scale with SPARC. While the first work beyond spreadsheets for the machine that would become SPARC started in October 2015, the design started in earnest in June 2018 when CFS was first funded. Conceptual design was completed in ..., the physics basis was published in ..., site groundbreaking was in ..., [other public milestones], and in early 2026 the project was considered 75% complete.

This is in contrast to China's BEST tokamak...

CFS has had to procure major parts of SPARC from international suppliers. Of those publicly listed include: [Fujikura](https://www.fujikura.co.jp/en/news/pressrelease/20250902cfs.html) in Japan for superconductor (Fujikura also is an investor in CFS); [Faraday Factory](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/brandon-sorbom-abb0ba14a_high-temperature-superconductors-hts-are-activity-7337835387534409728-42en) in Japan for superconductor; [SuperOx](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-81559-z) in Japan and Russia for superconductor; [ATB Riva Calzoni](https://www.atb.group/en/mag/2025-01-atb-group-for-clean-fusion-energy.html) of Italy for the cryostat base (very large and precise metal structure); [Walter Tosto S.p.A.](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mumgaard_tough-tech-on-display-this-is-sparcs-airtight-activity-7193323643714650113-kSyo) in Italy for the vacuum vessel (another very large and precise metal structure); [SIMIC S.p.A.](https://www.simic.it/2024/07/09/we-are-producing-cases-for-the-toroidal-field-coils-of-sparc-magnets/) in Italy for the toroidal field magnet cases (again, very large and precise metal structures).

Of particular note for those interested in geopolitics: SuperOx Japan was reorganized and rebranded in 2022 under U.S.-based Faraday 1867 Holdings. [Faraday materials state that Faraday Factory Japan was founded in July 2011, previously operated as “SuperOx Japan LLC (July 2011 - Dec 2022),” and was owned by “Faraday 1867 Holding LLC, USA” from July 2022](https://snf.ieeecsc.org/files/ieeecsc/slides/Lee%20presentation-min.pdf). Faraday’s stated rationale centers on commercial scale-up: [the company describes its business as R&D, manufacturing, and sales of HTS tape](https://www.faradaygroup.com/en/company/), and [its 2023 presentation says its mission is to serve the world market with high-quality 2G HTS tape at large volume and affordable cost](https://snf.ieeecsc.org/files/ieeecsc/slides/Lee%20presentation-min.pdf). The timing is to be noted: [Axios reported in March 2022 that CFS used HTS tape from SuperOx, described SuperOx as a Russian company with production facilities in Moscow and Japan, and reported that CFS was diversifying HTS supply after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine added urgency](https://www.axios.com/2022/03/21/sanctions-threaten-commonwealth-supply-chain). But public sources do not show that Faraday said the rebrand had anything to do with Russia's invasion. [The SEC Form D for Faraday 1867 Holdings LLC lists it as a Delaware LLC formed in 2022 and lists SuperOx founder Andrey Vavilov as a related person, executive officer, and member](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1932858/000110465922080818/xslFormDX01/primary_doc.xml).

CFETR was China’s declared post-ITER engineering step. In the early roadmap, [MOST had founded a national integration design group for CFETR in 2011](https://fire.pppl.gov/FPA14_Chinese_CFETR_Wan.pdf), and China hoped to [start CFETR construction in 2020 and complete it in 2030 with roughly 200 MW of fusion power, steady-state-operation testing, and tritium self-sustainment tests](https://fire.pppl.gov/FPA14_Chinese_CFETR_Wan.pdf). The same roadmap put a large share of the reactor problem into CFETR’s first phase: [Q = 1–5, steady state, TBR > 1, more than 200 MW of fusion power, and 10 dpa](https://fire.pppl.gov/FPA14_Chinese_CFETR_Wan.pdf). As the program matured, that package looked too large for China’s first domestic D-T step. Later CFETR/CFEDR presentations still kept the same reactor mission—[steady-state operation, high duty factor, tritium self-sufficiency, breeding blankets, remote handling, licensing and safety, and high fusion power at power-plant-relevant scale](https://indico.euro-fusion.org/event/3045/contributions/11876/attachments/5506/9655/CFETR-Eurofusion%20Workshop_20240203%28VC%29.pdf)—but the schedule and risk profile demanded an intermediate step.

CRAFT was the answer to the engineering gap. China’s 13th Five-Year Plan for major science infrastructure listed the [“Comprehensive Research Facility for Key Systems of a Fusion Reactor Main Machine” as a priority facility](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/xxgk/zcfb/ghwb/201701/W020190905497895917256.pdf), and the Hefei project became CRAFT: a fusion-technology campus built to mature the hard subsystems before a reactor-scale machine. CAS’s Hefei Institutes describe CRAFT as a [comprehensive testbench with reactor-relevant parameters for magnets, heating systems, divertors, and important materials](https://english.hf.cas.cn/nr/bth/202203/t20220328_303131.html). The same CAS account shows the practical buildout: [groundbreaking on December 14, 2018](https://english.hf.cas.cn/nr/bth/202203/t20220328_303131.html), construction progress through 2019–2021, and [site-infrastructure completion in 2022](https://english.hf.cas.cn/nr/bth/202203/t20220328_303131.html). CRAFT turned CFETR from a design ambition into a hardware-development program, with Hefei as the center of gravity for magnets, divertors, heating, materials, cryogenics, tritium-relevant systems, and reactor integration.

BEST then pulled the burning-plasma milestone forward. ASIPP publicly described BEST in 2021 as a [new planned D-T device to support CFETR with lower risk and cost](https://firefusionpower.org/FPA21-2_CN_ASIPP_Fusion_Song.pdf), and a 2024 CFETR/EUROfusion presentation made the gap explicit: after listing ITER, EAST, CFETR, and CRAFT, it asked, [“What to fill the gap? A new tokamak!”](https://indico.euro-fusion.org/event/3045/contributions/11876/attachments/5506/9655/CFETR-Eurofusion%20Workshop_20240203%28VC%29.pdf). The 2025 BEST Research Plan describes ASIPP’s stepwise strategy toward CFEDR as including [the CRAFT technology campus and the new superconducting tokamak BEST](https://euro-fusion.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/BEST-Research-Plan-v1.1.pdf). BEST is designed as a [new superconducting D-T tokamak with a full-tungsten first wall and divertor, R0 = 3.6 m, a = 1.1 m, B0 = 6.15 T, plasma current up to 7 MA, about 50 MW of auxiliary heating, and a 110 g tritium inventory](https://euro-fusion.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/BEST-Research-Plan-v1.1.pdf). Its mission is to [achieve Q ≥ 1, develop long-pulse D-T operation and integrated fusion-technology applications, and explore alpha-particle and burning-plasma physics around Q ≈ 5](https://euro-fusion.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/BEST-Research-Plan-v1.1.pdf). The sequence is therefore clear: CFETR defined the reactor objective, CRAFT built the enabling technology base, and BEST became the nearer D-T bridge meant to de-risk CFETR/CFEDR before China attempts the larger reactor step.

## Middling: circuits/chips

1986–1990 — [Seventh Five-Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/200709/P020191029595676468654.pdf)
“Bring integrated circuits ... up to a relatively advanced world level.”

1991–1995 — [Eighth Five-Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/200709/P020191029595681819982.pdf)
“... strengthen the development of integrated circuits ... actively develop microelectronics ... technology and equipment ... strengthen the R&D and production of electronic components, specialized high-precision equipment and instruments, and specialized materials, so as to improve product performance and quality.”

1996–2000 — [Ninth Five-Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/200709/P020191029595686994247.pdf)
“Accelerate the industrialization of high technology, focusing on ... submicron integrated circuits and 8-inch single-crystal silicon manufacturing technology ... and strive to improve the performance, quality, and market share of high-tech products.”

2001–2005 — [Tenth Five](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjjzxgh/200507/W020191104623866254322.pdf)-[Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/200709/P020191029595691974319.pdf)
“Vigorously develop the integrated-circuit ... industries ... Establish an integrated-circuit industrial system and pursue leapfrog development ... vigorously develop IC design, chip manufacturing, and testing, form supporting production and service systems for integrated circuits, and also develop IC equipment manufacturing.”

2006–2010 — [Eleventh Five-Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjjzxgh/200709/P020191104623156010398.pdf)
“Strengthen innovation capacity in the integrated-circuit industry ... accelerate the R&D and industrialization of 90nm-and-below IC manufacturing process technologies ... achieve breakthroughs in some key equipment and materials, and form a relatively complete IC industrial chain led by design, centered on manufacturing, and supported by equipment manufacturing and ancillary industries.”

2011–2015 — [Twelfth Five](https://www.most.gov.cn/xxgk/xinxifenlei/gjkjgh/201107/t20110713_88230.html)-[Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/201109/P020191029595702423333.pdf)
“... priority development areas include ... integrated circuits ... Strengthen R&D in key information-industry technologies ... with breakthroughs in ... integrated circuits and key components ...”

2016–2020 — [Thirteenth](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjfzgh/201603/P020191104614882474091.pdf) [Five-Year](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjjzxgh/201705/t20170508_1196736.html) [Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/fggz/fzzlgh/gjjzxgh/201705/t20170502_1196729.html)
“Foster an integrated-circuit industrial system ... Accelerate the industrialization of 16/14nm processes and the construction of memory production lines ... and promote the rapid development of packaging and testing, key equipment, materials, and related industries ... Achieve breakthroughs in key core technologies such as high-end general-purpose chips, integrated-circuit equipment, and foundational software ... and break through in electronic design automation (EDA) software.”

2021–2025 — [Fourteenth](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/xxgk/zcfb/ghwb/202103/P020210313315693279320.pdf) [Five-Year Plan](https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/xxgk/zcfb/ghwb/202103/P020210313315693279320.pdf)
"Focus on key areas such as high-end chips ... and accelerate breakthroughs and iterative application in fundamental theory, basic algorithms, equipment, and materials ... Implement the industrial-foundation reengineering program and accelerate efforts to shore up bottlenecks and weak links in ... foundational software, foundational materials, and related areas ... Promote innovative development in industries including integrated circuits ...”

2026–2030 — [Fifteenth Five-Year Plan draft](https://wb.beijing.gov.cn/home/index/wsjx/202603/t20260316_4557635.html)
"Promote, across the full chain, decisive breakthroughs in key core technologies in priority fields including integrated circuits ...”

        .range([topPad, topPad + (END_YEAR - START_YEAR) * pixelsPerYear]);

      let maxBottom = rawScale(END_YEAR);

      laneItems(singleColumn).forEach(function (items) {
        let previousBottom = -Infinity;

        items.forEach(function (item) {
          const rawY = rawScale(item.year);
          let top = rawY - (item.cardHeight / 2);

          if (top < previousBottom + packGap) {
            top = previousBottom + packGap;
          }

          item.top = top;
          item.y = top + (item.cardHeight / 2);
          item.bottom = top + item.cardHeight;
          previousBottom = item.bottom;
          maxBottom = Math.max(maxBottom, item.bottom);
        });
      });

      const height = Math.ceil(maxBottom + bottomPad);
      root.style.height = height + "px";
      cardsLayer.style("height", height + "px");

      events.style("transform", function (d) {
        return "translateY(" + Math.round(d.top) + "px)";
      });

      const ticks = d3.range(START_YEAR, END_YEAR + 0.0001, TICK_STEP);

      svg
        .attr("width", width)
        .attr("height", height)
        .attr("viewBox", "0 0 " + width + " " + height)
        .attr("preserveAspectRatio", "xMidYMin meet");

      svg.selectAll("*").remove();

      svg.append("line")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__axis")
        .attr("x1", axisX)
        .attr("x2", axisX)
        .attr("y1", topPad)
        .attr("y2", height - bottomPad);

      svg.selectAll(".cf-timeline__connector")
        .data(data, function (d) { return d.id; })
        .join("line")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__connector")
        .attr("x1", axisX)
        .attr("x2", function (d) {
          if (singleColumn || d.side === "right") {
            return d.x;
          }
          return d.x + cardWidth;
        })
        .attr("y1", function (d) { return d.y; })
        .attr("y2", function (d) { return d.y; });

      svg.selectAll(".cf-timeline__event-dot")
        .data(data, function (d) { return d.id; })
        .join("circle")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__event-dot")
        .attr("cx", axisX)
        .attr("cy", function (d) { return d.y; })
        .attr("r", 4);

      const tickGroups = svg.selectAll(".cf-timeline__tick")
        .data(ticks)
        .join("g")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__tick")
        .attr("transform", function (d) {
          return "translate(" + axisX + "," + rawScale(d) + ")";
        });

      tickGroups.append("circle")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__tick-circle")
        .attr("r", 6);

      tickGroups.append("text")
        .attr("class", "cf-timeline__tick-label")
        .attr("x", singleColumn ? 8 : -18)
        .attr("y", singleColumn ? -10 : 4)
        .attr("text-anchor", singleColumn ? "start" : "end")
        .text(function (d) { return d; });
    }

    layout();

    let raf = null;
    const rerender = function () {
      if (raf) cancelAnimationFrame(raf);
      raf = requestAnimationFrame(layout);
    };

    if (typeof ResizeObserver !== "undefined") {
      const observer = new ResizeObserver(rerender);
      observer.observe(root);
    } else {
      window.addEventListener("resize", rerender);
    }
  }

  loadD3()
    .then(renderTimeline)
    .catch(function () {
      root.innerHTML = '<div class="cf-timeline__error">The interactive D3 timeline could not load because the D3 script failed to download.</div>';
    });
})();
</script>
