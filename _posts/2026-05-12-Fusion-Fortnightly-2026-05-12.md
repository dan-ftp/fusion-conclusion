*No fluff, all facts.*

[Type One Energy, Tokamak Energy, and AECOM form the UK Infinity Fusion Consortium](https://www.world-nuclear-news.org/articles/british-us-consortium-aims-to-build-uk-fusion-plant). On May 6, the three companies said they had formed a consortium to pursue what they describe as the first private-sector-led fusion power plant project in the UK. The new development is the combination of a 400 MWe stellarator design, UK high-temperature-superconductor capability, and heavyweight engineering and construction execution into a specific deployment vehicle that builds on the Tennessee Valley Authority pathway in the US. That matters because it gives UK suppliers, financiers, siting partners, and possible offtakers a clearer commercial route beyond the state-led STEP program, though no final financing, site, or offtake terms were announced.

[South Korea launches work on its fifth basic fusion-energy plan](https://www.yna.co.kr/view/AKR20260507101400017). On May 7, Seoul’s science ministry kicked off the statutory planning process for its 2027–2031 fusion strategy, with a 56-member committee and a stated goal of demonstrating fusion power generation in the 2030s. What is genuinely new is the move from broad ambition to a formal plan covering KSTAR 2.0 upgrades, an AI virtual reactor, private-sector ecosystem building, workforce, international cooperation, and regulation, with a draft due this year. That matters because statutory planning is how budgets, infrastructure priorities, and industrial roles get defined for Korean suppliers, utilities, and policymakers.

[DOE and Max Planck lock in a new 10-year W7-X partnership](https://www.pppl.gov/news/2026/transatlantic-fusion-energy-research-just-got-easier). A Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory account published on April 28 said the US Department of Energy and Germany’s Max Planck Institute for Plasma Physics had signed a decade-long project agreement for Wendelstein 7-X. The new point is not just the duration but the fact that W7-X is the first project established under a new US-EU model framework for joint fusion research. That matters because stellarators are the leading alternative to tokamaks, and long-horizon public backing improves access to shared diagnostics, data, hardware, and credibility for organizations building around that path.

[Zap Energy adds fission to its strategy](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/29/fusion-power-startup-zap-energy-pulls-a-partial-pivot-adding-nuclear-fission-to-the-mix/). On April 29, Zap said it would build fission reactors alongside its fusion work, a sharp strategic pivot for one of the better-funded US fusion developers. The genuinely new development is the shift from a pure fusion story to an integrated nuclear platform meant to exploit overlap in materials, liquid metals, neutron environments, and nearer-term revenue opportunities. That matters because it changes how investors, suppliers, and partners assess Zap’s risk, financing durability, and competitive position, even though it does not by itself improve Zap’s fusion physics.

[ITER says its turnaround has put the project back on budget and on schedule](https://www.nucnet.org/news/iter-european-fusion-project-now-on-budget-and-on-schedule-after-turnaround-5-5-2026). On May 8, NucNet reported ITER management saying the project had entered a critical assembly phase and was now on budget and on schedule after its turnaround effort. What is new is the claimed restoration of cost and schedule discipline as assembly intensifies, a notable contrast with the delay-reset narrative of the past two years. That matters because ITER remains the largest source of reactor-scale engineering lessons for public labs and private suppliers, though this is management reporting rather than an outside audit.

[Chinese researchers report a one-minute high-performance tokamak regime with lower heat loads](https://phys.org/news/2026-04-tokamak-regime-sustains-stable-fusion.html). A Phys.org report published on April 29 highlighted a new Physical Review Letters result from China’s EAST team: a minute-long operating regime that combined partial divertor detachment, ELM-free H-mode, and high pedestal performance in a metal-wall tokamak. What is new is the simultaneous handling of two of fusion’s hardest engineering problems — heat exhaust and confinement quality — in a single long-pulse regime. That matters because reactor economics and uptime depend on surviving heat loads without wrecking plasma performance, although this remains a physics milestone rather than a plant-scale commercial proof.

[Startorus Fusion closes a 500 million yuan A+ round and crosses unicorn status](https://finance.eastmoney.com/a/202605073730027724.html). Chinese financial media reported on May 7 that Shanghai-based Startorus Fusion had raised 500 million yuan in an A+ round, taking cumulative funding above 2 billion yuan and valuation above $1 billion. The new development is not just another early-stage raise: the money is earmarked for Jiading base construction, NTST installation and operation, reactor-design iteration, fusion-grade high-temperature-superconductor magnet production, and AI plasma-control engineering, only months after a 1 billion yuan Series A. That matters because it suggests China’s private fusion market is moving from research teams toward capitalized engineering programs, though the report comes through Chinese media rather than a global wire and funding alone does not prove execution.

[Fusion Futures Industry Capability Report 25/26: building the means to power fusion](https://www.ukaea.org/news/fusion-futures-industry-capability-report-25-26/). UKAEA says its Fusion Futures Industry Capability report records significant progress in enhancing UK supply-chain capability, with more than 200 industry partners engaged, hundreds of contracts awarded to UK companies, and more than 75% of technical programme spending allocated to the supply chain. The page says the funded work covers plasma control systems, robotics, diagnostics, advanced manufacturing, and tritium fuel handling, directly tying public spending to named fusion-relevant technology areas and suppliers.

[Celebrating Fusion Futures: 2024 to 2026 report showcases major achievements](https://www.ukaea.org/news/celebrating-fusion-futures-2024-to-2026-report-highlights-major-achievements/). UKAEA says its end-of-year Fusion Futures report covers progress in research, infrastructure, skills, and innovation, including LIBRTI as a breeder-blanket test facility and added UK expertise in handling and decommissioning tritium-contaminated materials. The page also says the Industry Capability programme has supported hundreds of suppliers and funded SMEs working on remote handling systems, liquid metal loops, advanced manufacturing techniques, and high-temperature testing capabilities.

[UK Fusion Energy strengthens Korea partnership to scale fusion cable manufacturing](https://stepfusion.com/uk-fusion-energy-strengthens-korea-partnership/). UK Fusion Energy says it has expanded its partnership with Seoul National University and PowerNix to scale high-temperature superconducting cable manufacturing for fusion magnets, with an additional £5 million from UKFE for materials, tools, and equipment and £1.1 million of further in-kind contributions from SNU and PowerNix. The page says this next phase will prototype the first pancake for the STEP toroidal-field model coil, scale manufacturing to 10-meter cable lengths, and advance cable bending, production automation, testing, and inspection, with Standard Magnet participating as a subcontractor.

[Helion Energy is building “Tiny Merge,” a new fusion testbed less than one-eighth the size of Polaris](https://www.geekwire.com/2026/helion-makes-big-bet-on-tiny-merge-fusion-testbed-to-meet-aggressive-microsoft-timeline/). The report said Helion will use the smaller machine to test plasma behavior, merging dynamics, and magnet ideas faster and more cheaply than on full-scale devices, with a goal of starting it up by the end of the summer. The article framed the move as a material design-and-development update as Helion works toward its 2028 power-delivery timeline.

[General Fusion announced a DOE-funded peer-reviewed fuel-cycle study with Savannah River National Laboratory](https://generalfusion.com/post/u-s-department-of-energy-funded-study-supports-general-fusions-fuel-cycle-design/). General Fusion said the work, published in *Fusion Science and Technology* under DOE’s INFUSE program, supports the tritium-supply and fuel-sustainability case for its Magnetized Target Fusion design and liquid-metal wall. The update is notable because fuel-cycle closure is one of the harder engineering constraints for commercial D-T fusion systems.

[Kyoto Fusioneering said in a joint research agreement announcement that it had signed with Starlight Engine and the University of Tokyo to study divertor plasma for the FAST project](https://kyotofusioneering.com/en/news/2026/04/30/3915). Kyoto Fusioneering said the work will use one-dimensional plasma transport simulations to evaluate heat loads, particle transport, and plasma-wall interactions, with the aim of establishing divertor designs and operating strategies suited to FAST. The FAST program is intended to demonstrate fusion power generation in the 2030s, so the agreement is a concrete subsystem-design step rather than a generic research tie-up.

[Zap Energy announced that it had appointed Zabrina Johal as CEO and was formalizing an integrated nuclear strategy spanning both fission and fusion](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/zap-energy-advances-integrated-nuclear-strategy-and-appoints-zabrina-johal-as-ceo-302757297.html). Zap Energy said cofounder Benj Conway will become president and focus on strategy, partnerships, and long-term technology development. The release said the company’s new plan is to pair nearer-term fission deployment with the longer-term breakthrough potential of its fusion platform.

[nT-Tao announced that it had signed an MoU with Israel’s national water company to explore fusion energy for critical water infrastructure](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/nt-tao-and-israels-national-water-company-mekorot-partner-to-explore-fusion-energy-for-critical-water-infrastructure-302756616.html). nT-Tao said the phased collaboration will start in 2026 with technical and feasibility assessments toward a dedicated R&D center, alongside early business and design alignment under a Strategic Design Partner framework. The intended use cases include desalination and wastewater systems, making the announcement a concrete customer-side infrastructure test for fusion deployment.
