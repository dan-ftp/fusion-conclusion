## Companies

[General Fusion completes deSPAC with Spring Valley Acquisition Corp. III](https://generalfusion.com/post/general-fusion-completes-business-combination-with-spring-valley-acquisition-corp-iii/). General Fusion began trading on Nasdaq on July 13 under [GFUZ](https://www.nasdaq.com/market-activity/stocks/gfuz).

[General Fusion files SEC Form 20-F with details on how the deSPAC closed](https://www.sec.gov/ix?doc=/Archives/edgar/data/2074850/000110465926084015/tm2620136d1_20f.htm). The main news is that 91.6% of the SPAC investors chose to take their money back instead of keeping shares, redeeming about $217m. General Fusion still ended up with about $170m in cash, most of which came from private investors and money the company already had. The private investors got a very favorable deal: a 12% annual return that can be added to what General Fusion owes them, plus warrants that let them buy more shares at $12 if the stock’s value goes up. If General Fusion’s stock falls or the company later raises money at a lower price, those investors can receive more shares, diluting ordinary shareholders. The lead investor also received 3.5m shares for almost nothing. After five years, these investors can ask General Fusion to repay their preferred shares in cash, which could exceed $225m. The deSPAC bought General Fusion time, but at a price: future shareholders face substantial dilution, and the company may need another major financing or refinancing by July 2031.

[Realta Fusion announced that it is moving its headquarters to the former Oscar Mayer plant in Madison](https://wedc.org/realta-fusion-to-relocate-corporate-headquarters-to-former-oscar-mayer-plant-in-madison/). The city and state have committed up to $55M to support this move, including tax exemptions and credits. Next week, CFS will announce its relocation to the Dunkin’ Donuts bakery and Proxima to the Rischart Bavarian bakery. Who’s next to move to a facility that made food in the shape of its plasma?

[Commonwealth Fusion Systems posted a SPARC project update](https://blog.cfs.energy/cfs-prepares-sparcs-fusion-support-systems-for-dress-rehearsal/). CFS said its cryogenic plant is operating and circulating cryogenic fluid through magnet-cooling subsystems, and that the upcoming “dry dress rehearsal” will run the plant as if it were powering a fusion pulse while tokamak assembly continues in parallel. Standard plant commissioning work: bringing systems online for operation.

[StandardX comes out of stealth](https://www.standardx.tech/). From Kyoto Fusioneering co-founder [Richard Pearson](https://www.linkedin.com/in/richard-pearson-phd-65660861/) and nuclear physicist [Ross Allen](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rossamallen/) comes “the world’s first isotope refinery: a single accelerator platform that produces the full portfolio of isotopes from one machine.” They plan on using a [fixed-field alternating-gradient accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed-field_alternating_gradient_accelerator). I am seeing a lot of companies planning on isotope production. See the StandardX website for the menu of isotopes they would like to sell to you, including tritium.

[Pacific Fusion’s pulsed-power prototype completes 3,000 shots](https://www.pacificfusion.com/updates/lawrence-livermore-pacific-fusion-collaboration-breaks-new-record-to-advance-americas-high-gain-fusion-capability). An important achievement in scaling up its system. At about 1 Hz for its power plant, 3,000 shots would take about an hour, so another factor of 10,000 is needed to reach a one-year-equivalent shot lifetime. I wonder whether reaching this rep rate was the major hurdle or whether a lot of effort will be needed to progress through the next couple of decades of shots.

## Government

[F4E publishes the first Global Fusion Energy Trends report](https://fusionforenergy.europa.eu/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/F4E_Observatory-Global_Fusion_Energy_Trends_1st_Edition.pdf). This is a really nice compilation of the state of fusion development internationally, covering China, the EU, Japan, the UK, and the US. There are really useful figures placing machines under construction in the context of what has already been achieved and showing timelines for planned facilities. It also shows data on things like publications and patents (China is patenting a lot, but only domestically!). Finally, there’s a comparison of regulatory timelines in the major regions. We’re seeing different strategies unfold in each region. Which one will be the most successful at developing fusion?

[China requests public comment on the draft “Safety Analysis Work Guideline for Deuterium-Tritium Fusion Experimental Devices”](https://www.mee.gov.cn/xxgk2018/xxgk/xxgk06/202607/t20260716_1161973.html).
