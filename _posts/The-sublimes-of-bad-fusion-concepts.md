# The sublimes of bad fusion concepts

[Bent Flyvbjerg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bent_Flyvbjerg) writes about the “sublimes” of megaprojects: the attractions that make giant projects irresistible even when the numbers do not work. Politicians like the ribbon cuttings. Engineers like the scale. Investors like the upside. The public likes the story.

Fusion has its own version of this problem.

The fusion industry is full of real science, real engineering, and real progress. It is also full of concepts that are much more attractive in pitch decks than they are in power balance. The danger is not that people are excited about fusion. They should be. The danger is that the excitement gets attached to the wrong thing.

Here are some of the sublimes that show up repeatedly in specious fusion energy concepts.

## The compactness sublime

The machine is small. That is the first seduction.

A compact fusion device sounds like the clean answer to tokamaks, lasers, tritium plants, remote maintenance, and giant power stations. Make it smaller and the costs come down. Make it simple and the timelines shrink. Make it fit in a room and fusion starts to look like a product instead of infrastructure.

The problem is that fusion does not reward smallness by itself. Compactness usually worsens wall loading, heat flux, neutron damage, shielding, stresses, maintenance, or some other term that was easier to ignore on the slide.

A small machine that cannot handle its losses is not a reactor. It is a small machine.

## The non-thermal sublime

This is the claim that the concept avoids the boring old Maxwellian plasma problem.

The ions are in orbits. The distribution is monoenergetic. The electrons are cold. The fusion products are recovered. The old limits do not apply.

Sometimes there is a real idea here. But nature is quite committed to ruining these plans. Particles collide. Electrons heat. Ions scatter. Distributions broaden. Instabilities grow. Walls emit and absorb particles.

A non-thermal fusion concept should be judged by how it handles entropy. Most are judged instead by how elegantly they avoid mentioning it.

## The aneutronic sublime

Aneutronic fusion is the clean-fusion dream: no tritium, fewer neutrons, less activation, simpler licensing, direct conversion, and no reactor that has to be rebuilt by robots.

It is easy to see the appeal. D+T fusion has miserable engineering attached to it. Tritium breeding is hard. Neutron damage is hard. Remote maintenance is hard.

But a harder fuel does not make the system easier. Proton-boron and other advanced fuels buy their attractions with much harsher plasma requirements: higher temperatures, better confinement, better impurity control, and tighter radiation-loss limits.

The right question is not whether aneutronic fusion would be nice. It would be. The question is whether the plasma can work before it glows away its power.

## The direct-conversion sublime

Direct conversion is one of the great fusion temptations.

Why make heat, boil water, spin a turbine, and accept thermal-cycle losses if charged fusion products can be converted directly into electricity?

The appeal is real. The problem is that direct conversion only helps after the reactor works. It does not solve confinement, bremsstrahlung, startup, parasitic power, wall loading, or plant availability.

It is also rarely as clean as the cartoon. Real particles scatter. Real plasmas radiate. Real collectors live in harsh fields and particle environments.

Direct conversion is a possible improvement to a reactor that already works. It is not a substitute for one.

## The simulation sublime

The plots are beautiful.

The particles stay confined. The fields look clean. The density profile holds together. The movie runs. The colors are convincing.

Simulations are essential to fusion. The problem is simulation as theater. For any impressive result, the first question is what was left out: collisions, electrons, sheaths, impurities, charge exchange, electrode emission, 3D geometry, realistic boundaries, or long-time losses.

All models are wrong. Some are useful. The least useful model is the one that works because it excludes the physics that would kill the concept.

## The Rider-escape sublime

This is the most revealing one.

The pitch says the usual fusion limits do not apply. Rider assumed the wrong distribution. The plasma is not Maxwellian. The ions are not thermal. The electrons are not hot. The energy is recovered. The old papers missed the clever part.

Maybe. But then show it.

A serious concept should state exactly which assumption it rejects, what replaces it, and what the new power balance is. It should quantify relaxation, radiation, particle losses, wall losses, recirculating power, and recovery efficiency.

The weak version simply says “Rider does not apply” and moves on. That is not an argument. That is a warning label.

## The beautiful-machine sublime

Some fusion concepts look like they should work.

They have clean symmetry, clever electrodes, elegant rings, levitated coils, dramatic pulses, or glowing plasmas in small chambers. They photograph well. They animate well.

Aesthetic appeal is not meaningless. Good engineering often has a kind of elegance to it. But fusion is full of attractive machines with ugly power balances.

The plasma does not care whether the CAD looks clean. Neutrons do not care whether the chamber is symmetric. Heat flux does not care whether the device photographs well.

The machine can be beautiful and still be wrong.

## The founder sublime

Fusion investors are highly vulnerable to the founder story.

The outsider genius. The ignored physicist. The former national-lab scientist. The repeat entrepreneur. The person who saw what the whole establishment missed.

Sometimes the establishment does miss things. But the founder story is not evidence.

The test is whether the concept survives quantitative review by people who know the relevant physics and have no need to be polite. Can the founder defend the power balance, confinement assumptions, diagnostics, and reactor path?

“They laughed at the Wright brothers” is not a confinement scheme.

## The secrecy sublime

A company says the key breakthrough is proprietary.

That can be reasonable. Fusion companies should protect intellectual property. They should not publish every design detail.

But secrecy becomes a problem when it shields the central claim from examination. A company does not need to disclose everything to be credible. It does need to disclose enough for the basic physics to be checked: temperature, density, confinement time, volume, input power, duty cycle, neutron rate, losses, and measurement methods.

If the breakthrough cannot be evaluated without the secret sauce, the default assumption should be that the sauce is doing too much work.

## The milestone sublime

Fusion milestones are easy to make sound more important than they are.

Plasma achieved. Neutrons detected. First light. Record field. Net electricity path. Licensed for neutron production. Commercial prototype announced.

Some of these are real accomplishments. Many are worth celebrating. But most do not answer the reactor question.

A neutron count can be real and still be irrelevant. A plasma can be beautiful and still be far from fusion conditions. A high field can be impressive and still not make a plant.

The right question is always: what changed in the integrated path to useful power?

If the answer is nothing measurable in the power balance, the milestone is probably marketing.

## The climate-rescue sublime

Fusion is important because energy is important.

A cheap, clean, scalable fusion power plant would be one of the most important technologies ever built. It could help with electricity, industrial heat, desalination, fuels, shipping, and energy security.

That is exactly why judgment gets clouded.

The more people want a technology to exist, the easier it is to lower the standard of proof. Climate urgency can turn weak evidence into “worth a shot.” It can make technical criticism sound like pessimism.

Fusion should be pursued aggressively because the prize is enormous. That does not mean every fusion concept deserves belief.

## The national-security sublime

Fusion also has a strategic story.

Energy independence. Industrial strength. Defense applications. Isotopes. Naval power. Space power. Competition with China. Control of the next great energy industry.

Much of this is legitimate. Fusion is strategically important.

But “important if true” is not the same as “likely to work.” National-security framing can attract funding and protect weak concepts from normal scrutiny.

A bad reactor concept does not become good because it has a flag next to it.

## The capital-market sublime

Fusion has a trillion-dollar story.

If commercial fusion works, the market is enormous. That makes even low-probability bets look rational. A venture investor does not need certainty. They need asymmetric upside.

This can be true for the investor and misleading for everyone else.

A small option on a miracle may be a reasonable financial bet. That does not make the miracle likely. The expected-value calculation for a fund is not the engineering case for a reactor.

## The incumbent-failure sublime

This one is especially common among alternative concepts.

Tokamaks are too big. ITER is too slow. NIF is not a power plant. Government programs are bureaucratic. Stellarators are too complex. Magnets are expensive. Lasers are inefficient.

Much of this is true or at least directionally fair.

But a critique of the incumbent is not validation of the alternative. ITER being slow does not make an electrostatic concept work. NIF not being a power plant does not make a tabletop fusion plant credible.

The alternative still has to close on its own merits.

## The single-bottleneck sublime

Bad fusion stories often reduce the problem to one bottleneck.

The magnets are the issue. The laser is the issue. The fuel is the issue. The regulation is the issue. The software is the issue. The materials are the issue.

Real fusion plants do not work like that.

Fusion is a coupled system. Higher field helps confinement but increases stress and shielding challenges. Smaller size reduces capital cost but increases wall loading. Higher density improves fusion power but increases collisionality and radiation.

There are no isolated miracles in a fusion power plant.

## The cheap-option sublime

Some concepts survive because the next experiment is cheap enough to fund.

For an investor, that may be reasonable. A $10 million or $20 million experiment can be a cheap way to buy optionality on a huge outcome. Even a long-shot concept can make financial sense.

But that logic should stay in its lane.

“Cheap to test” does not mean “likely to become a power plant.” It means cheap to test.

The fusion industry needs experiments. It also needs discipline in describing what those experiments prove.

## The conclusion

The common pattern is substitution.

A beautiful machine substitutes for a power balance. A clever distribution substitutes for a collisional calculation. A clean fuel substitutes for achievable plasma conditions. A simulation substitutes for a validated experiment. A milestone substitutes for reactor relevance. A founder story substitutes for technical evidence.

Fusion is hard enough when the concept is sound. It becomes impossible when the story is doing the work of the physics.

The test should be simple: after including major losses, realistic duty cycle, wall loading, materials damage, maintenance, fuel cycle, power conversion, and recirculating power, is there a credible path to useful electricity at plant scale?

If not, the concept may still be interesting science. It may still be a neutron source. It may still be a useful experiment. It may even be a reasonable venture option.

But it is not yet a credible fusion energy concept.

No matter how sublime it feels.
